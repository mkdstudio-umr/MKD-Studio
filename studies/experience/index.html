Experience

Before the event begins.

Events at scale are rarely documented from the inside. The photographs made of Formula One, of the World Cup, of professional tennis arrive before or after the moment: the warm-up, the ceremony, the press image made for a specific purpose. These studies were made from inside, with access few photographers have and a question none of the official photographers were asking: what does all of this actually feel like before it begins.

A Formula One paddock in the hours before a race session has a particular quality of time. The cars are in their garages. The engineers are working. Everything is moving toward a fixed point, and the infrastructure assembled around that point is temporary, total and visible only to a small number of people. The study made at Silverstone looked at that infrastructure: not the racing but the world built around it, which disappears the moment it ends.

The other studies here work by the same logic. The FIFA World Cup in Qatar, 2022: a host nation at full capacity, photographed not for the matches but for the experience of being inside an event of that scale. A golf club at dusk. A tennis tournament in a garden. The interest in each case is not the sport. It is the temporary world built to contain it: the marquees, the protocols, the quality of light at the moment just before it all begins.

These are temporary worlds. They are assembled for a few days, sometimes a few weeks, then they are gone. The photographs are what remain.

Two of the Experience studies have continued as editorial publications, expanding beyond the study format into something that can be read rather than browsed.
