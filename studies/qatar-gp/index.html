Qatar Grand Prix

Lusail, under the lights
Setting

Lusail is designed for a single moment. A circuit waiting beneath the desert sky until the lights come on and the grandstands begin to fill. For one weekend the rhythm changes. The movement starts long before the race itself.

Anticipation

The cars were never the subject. The photographs found the edges of the event: the anticipation before a session, the movement of people through a space built for one weekend a year, the glow of hospitality suites after sunset, the way the circuit changed character as day became night. The race was only one part of the atmosphere.

Movement

The frames that hold are the ones from after dark, when the circuit came alive under the lights and the noise and the heat all arrived at once.

There is a version of a Grand Prix that is pure event coverage. This was not that. The interest was in what it feels like to be inside the machine: the passes, the paddock, the precision of a team preparing the car in the time between sessions. Formula One is a great deal of very controlled effort, rendered briefly visible.

Response

The circuit holds 1.28 million people across a race weekend. The number is real, and it means nothing in the moment of being there.

What the photographs hold is not the scale but the texture: a particular hour of light, a quality of noise and heat and movement after dark, the way a temporary city assembles itself around a few hours of competition and then dissolves. Lusail, under the lights, is the clearest version of what Formula One has decided to be.
