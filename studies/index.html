Editorial Studies

The Studies.

Each study is a record of a place and the feeling it left behind. Not a portfolio and not a travel diary. An archive of sustained attention, built from repeated visits, extended hours and the discipline of looking at a subject until it reveals something that a single afternoon would not.

The archive spans four worlds: hospitality, places, craft and experience. From hotel lobbies in Doha to spice markets in Tangier, Formula One paddocks to fragrance houses in Milan, across four continents. Each made independently, without a brief, because the subject seemed worth the attention.

New studies join the archive as they are made.

Hospitality

Hotels, cafés and restaurants are remembered long after check-out.

Study /

KAAWA, Manchester

Light after dark

Some places are remembered for years and it is difficult to explain why. Not the view or the price or the reputation. Something quieter. A particular light at a particular hour. The feeling that the room was designed for a version of you that knew how to slow down.

The best hospitality rarely announces itself. It simply makes everything feel slightly more considered than it needed to be. A lobby that is calm before the day begins. A restaurant that earns its atmosphere rather than decorating for it. A hotel that understands the difference between service and theatre.

What separates the memorable from the merely competent is almost never the obvious things. It is in the decision to use a particular material at a certain height. The choice to leave space where another hotel would have filled it. The understanding that a guest does not want to be managed, only looked after. These are not design principles. They are instincts, and the places that have them tend to know it.

These studies look at what a place feels like, not what it offers.

Places

Cities reveal themselves gradually.

Study /

Copenhagen

The rhythm of an ordinary day

Every city has an official version of itself. The landmark, the itinerary, the photograph that already exists on a thousand other cameras. These studies begin where that version runs out.

What a place actually feels like is rarely found in its headline attractions. It is found in the peripheral streets, the hour before things open, the interior that tourists walk past on the way to somewhere else. The atmosphere that a place has when it is not performing for anyone in particular.

A city also changes depending on how slowly you move through it. The things that reveal themselves over an hour are different from the things visible in an afternoon. Most travel photography is made at pace, between one destination and the next. These studies were made differently. Extended time in a single place, returning to the same streets at different hours, learning what a city looks like when nothing particular is happening.

Craft

Objects become meaningful through attention.

Study /

The Penny Bun, Askwith

A restored inn and the pace of a Yorkshire morning

The most considered spaces rarely ask for your attention directly. They earn it through accumulation. The material chosen for a surface. The proportion of a shelf. The way light moves through a room at a particular hour. These are decisions that most people will not consciously notice, and that is precisely the point.

Craft, in this context, is not about price or heritage. It is about the degree of thought built into a space. The fragrance house photographed through its objects and spaces rather than its bottles. The café that was once a palace and treats the room accordingly. The retail interior built to be inhabited rather than displayed.

There is a category of brand that understands its physical space as an argument. Not a backdrop for the product, but an extension of it. The room makes a claim about what the brand believes, and that claim either holds or it does not. These studies are interested in the ones that hold, and in what it actually looks like when a space is built from conviction rather than budget.

These studies look for the point of view built into the object and the space around it.

Experience

Atmosphere begins before the event itself.

Study /

HOST

Qatar, 2022

The event itself takes up most of the frame. The result, the performance, the moment that everyone came to see. These studies look at what surrounds it, which is where most of the atmosphere actually lives.

The hour before the gates open. The paddock at the point when the machinery has not yet started. The grandstand filling from the outside in. The hospitality tent where the conversation is still about everything except what is about to happen. Spectacle at scale has a particular texture to it, and it is almost never the obvious one.

Large events share a quality that is hard to describe from the outside. The sense that something is about to happen, distributed across thousands of people waiting for the same thing at the same time. That collective anticipation has its own atmosphere. It shows up in the way people move, the way they position themselves, the things they photograph before the main event begins.

These studies document the feeling of being inside something large, from the edges of the frame inward.
